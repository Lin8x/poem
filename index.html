The Version of Me That Died First

I was softer then. Before silence filled the shape of your voice. Before nights made everything louder. Before the boy you knew had to become something else just to survive waking up. You died, and I didn’t. But something inside me did. And I don’t know what to call it except before. I used to laugh without checking. Speak without rehearsing. Move without hearing that whisper that says— you’re not safe here. You’re not enough anymore. Grief doesn’t just take. It remakes. I see it in my reflection, how I’ve grown into someone you wouldn’t recognize. Not because I’ve changed, but because you’re not here to remember me the way I was when I was still innocent, still brave, still whole. I walk through our house like it’s been abandoned by time. Everything remains— the couches, the frames, even mom’s voice, still warm, still trying— but I feel like a ghost passing through comfort like it’s made of glass. I want to go back. But even if I could, I’d ruin it just by knowing what’s coming. Some mornings I wake and feel nothing. Not pain, not healing— just the dull gravity of another day survived. I wonder if you’d be proud. Of how I keep moving. Of how I still eat at places we used to go, even when the booths feel haunted and the food tastes like silence. There are no more words left between us. Just the way I sit in your absence, quietly. How I carry the past like a backpack full of sand— never visible, but always heavy. But still, I wake. And I act. And I love. Not because the weight is gone, but because there’s something in choosing to carry it— in making something from what remains. That is not peace. It’s not even hope. But it’s forward. And maybe that is enough.
